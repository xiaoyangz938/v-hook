Abstract
Vibration is a lightweight and widely available actuation source,
yet existing vibration-driven locomotion systems often rely on
specialized mechanisms, additional electronics, or extensive em-
pirical tuning, making them difficult to reproduce, adapt, and inte-
grate into everyday objects. To address this limitation, we present
V-Hook, a morphology-driven locomotion design approach that
encodes motion behavior through hook-shaped Morphologies fab-
ricated by FDM 3D printing. By leveraging asymmetric contact
and stick–slip interactions between the hooks and the supporting
surface, V-Hook transforms a uniform vibration input into distin-
guishable motion outputs. We systematically investigate how hook
morphology, density, length, and vibration conditions influence mo-
tion accuracy, stability, and load-bearing performance, and derive a
morphology-to-motion design space from these results. Building on
this foundation, we develop a design tool and online platform that
support motion authoring and sharing for lightweight interactive
objects, and validate the approach through a series of application
prototypes. By reframing vibration-driven motion as a problem of
hook morphology design, this work offers a new perspective for
integrating motion capabilities into digitally fabricated interactive
systems.

1 Introduction
Vibration is a lightweight and widely available actuation source [29,
39, 45] commonly found in mobile devices [6, 45], interactive arti-
facts [16], desktop tools [16, 31], and small robotic platforms [11, 34,
40]. In human-computer interaction, vibration has long been used
for haptic feedback [36–38] and notifications [1, 8, 15], but it also
offers an appealing pathway for motion generation: objects can be
driven without relying on wheels [14], joints [5], or complex trans-
mission mechanisms [19, 24]. For this reason, and compared with
systems based on gears [47], cables [23, 24], or pneumatics [3, 4, 44],
vibration is particularly suitable for small-scale, low-cost interactive
motion systems [31, 34, 45].
However, existing vibration-driven locomotion systems typically
achieve controllability through specialized mechanical designs [16],
additional control electronics [16, 31], or extensive empirical tun-
ing [20, 29]. As a result, motion behaviors are often tightly coupled
to specific device structures, making them difficult to reproduce,
transfer, or conveniently embed into everyday objects [7, 17, 25].
This challenge has limited designers and makers from bringing

vibration-driven motion into a broader range of HCI scenarios. Mo-
tivated by this challenge, we ask whether part of the control problem
can be shifted upstream into the fabrication stage itself. More specif-
ically, can low-cost 3D printing provide a low-complexity way of
authoring motion for lightweight objects, so thatvibration-driven
motion can be designed and controlled through structural
encoding rather than through the later addition of complex
control systems?
To address this question, we presentV-Hook, a morphology-
driven motion design approach that uses hook-shaped Morpholo-
gies fabricated by FDM 3D printing to encode motion behavior.
By leveraging asymmetric contact and stick–slip interactions be-
tween the hook structures and the supporting surface, V-Hook
transforms a uniform vibration input into distinguishable motion
outputs. Based on this mechanism, we further use G-code control
over extrusion amount and printing speed in FDM printing, to-
gether with gravity, fabrication strategies, and post-processing, to
construct four fundamental hook morphologies: full, tilted, half, and
double hooks. We systematically investigate how hook morphology,
along with density, length, and vibration conditions, influences mo-
tion accuracy, stability, and load-bearing performance. From these
results, and through further integration of printing strategies as well
as modular composition and assembly, we derive a morphology-to-
motion design space. To support broader accessibility, we develop a
design tool and online platform that support motion authoring and
sharing for lightweight interactive objects, and further demonstrate
the potential of this approach for motion-based interaction through
a series of interactive prototypes.
By reframing vibration-driven motion as a problem of hook mor-
phology design, this work offers a new perspective on integrating
motion capabilities into digitally fabricated interactive systems.
The contributions of this paper are as follows:
• We propose a fabrication-oriented motion primitive that
encodes controllable vibration-driven motion through 3D-
printed hook Morphologie s, by establishing a systematic
relationship between hook morphology, directional motion
behavior, and the factors that influence it.
• We develop a design tool and online platform that bridge
morphology and motion, supporting its use as an attachable
motion layer, a modular motion unit, and a motion design
component for lightweight, shareable interactive objects.
• Through a series of application prototypes, we demonstrate
the usability and potential of this approach for motion inter-
action.
2 Related Work
2.1 Vibration-driven Locomotion
Vibration-driven locomotion is commonly realized through inter-
nal vibration motors [34, 45], eccentric masses [29], piezoelectric
elements [12], or external vibration platforms [ 39]. In these sys-
tems, periodic excitation is applied to the structure as a whole [41],
and is converted into displacement through interactions with the
contact surface [27]. Such systems are attractive because they are
lightweight [34], easy to package [ 31], and require relatively lit-
tle mechanical assembly [45]. As a result, they have been widely
explored in low-cost robots [34] and interactive devices [29, 31].
Across robotics and human–computer interaction, vibration has
been used to enable sliding [31], crawling [9], bristle-based loco-
motion [13], and object movement [29, 45]. However, such systems
often support only a limited range of motion behaviors and re-
main difficult to control in a stable and practically usable manner.
For example,CARDinalityrelies on complex electronic systems
and a training process to learn usable motion,LayMoconstructs a
differential-friction steering mechanism through a specially compli-
ant mechanical structure, andRatchaircompensates for uncertainty
in vibration-driven motion through learning and optimization algo-
rithms [16, 29, 31]. Although these approaches improve controlla-
bility, they also substantially raise the barrier for users to reproduce
the system and extend it to richer motion patterns.
V-Hook takes a different path. Instead of adding more layers of
control, it introduces predictable directional bias at the levels of
fabrication and hook morphologies. In doing so, it makes vibration-
driven locomotion closer to a mechanism that can be directly de-
signed, parameterized, and combined, while also broadening the
range of motion behaviors that vibration can produce.
2.2 Morphology and 3D Printing for Encoding
Motion
In HCI and robotic systems, morphology is increasingly being
treated as an active medium for shaping behavior and motion [32],
rather than merely a passive shell for carrying actuators [35] and
control logic [30, 33, 44]. For example,McGeerproduces stable gaits
through leg Morphology and weight distribution alone, without
active actuation [26];Snake-Inspired Kirigami Skinuses kirigami
structures to create frictional anisotropy and thereby generate di-
rectional serpentine motion under uniform actuation [2];Capsular
Microrobotemploys a helical structure to transform rotational mo-
tion into linear propulsion [48]; andAgile Multimodal Microrobot
uses passively deformable wheels to switch among rolling, crawl-
ing, and climbing under a single drive mechanism [22]. Collectively,
these works suggest that complex behavior need not rely entirely
on fine-grained real-time control, but can instead be substantially
preconfigured by morphology.
With the rise of digital fabrication, and especially 3D printing,
this idea of “encoding behavior in morphology” has become in-
creasingly manufacturable in practice [17, 27, 42]. Morphology can
now be controlled with high precision [27, 42, 43], structures can
be rapidly iterated [7, 17], and behavior can therefore be more di-
rectly shaped through form [24, 47]. For example,Kinergyuses the
geometric design of 3D-printed springs to realize embedded kinetic
energy storage and support a variety of self-actuated motion be-
haviors [18];Cillliauses Digital Light Processing （DLP） printed
microstructures to control surface friction and guide the directional
movement of objects [28]. Yet in many such systems, the mapping
from printed form to final motion is still mediated by additional
structures, material properties, or assembly conditions, rather than
being directly encoded in the contact Morphology of the printed
object itself.
V-Hook addresses this gap through a lighter-weight fabrication
strategy. By printing hook-shaped structures using standard FDM
3D printers and activating them through vibration, it produces
diverse yet predictable motion behaviors. In this way, morphology

2.3 Computer-Aided Design Tools for
Customizable Motion Design
As motion behavior is increasingly shaped by Morphology [ 10,
17, 24], fabrication parameters [ 42, 43], and modular composi-
tion [17, 47], designers not only need to understand the relation-
ship between morphology and behavior, but also need tools that
support the generation, comparison, and reuse of such relation-
ships [7, 21, 25, 49]. Prior work has lowered the barrier to designing
complex Morphologies [42], fabrication workflows [21, 46], and
motion-generating mechanisms [24, 47] through design tools and
parametric workflows, and has further demonstrated their use in
everyday contexts [7, 25]. For instance,X-Stringsembeds tendon-
driven mechanisms into 3D-printed structures so that actuation,
deformation, and manipulation can be designed in an integrated
manner [24];Robiotautomatically generates printable actuation
mechanisms from target object behaviors [25]; andCompActfur-
ther supports the design of interconnected compliant mechanisms
around desired transmission goals, allowing structural generation
to respond more directly to intended motion behavior [47]. How-
ever, most existing tools focus on geometric modeling, fabrication
organization, or particular classes of structure generation, and do
not directly address the specific problem of encoding vibration-
driven motion through morphology.
Accordingly, this work further contributes a design tool and an
accompanying online platform that turn the mapping among target
motion, morphological parameters, and manufacturable structures
into a low-barrier, operable, and reusable design workflow.
3 V-Hook Overview
3.1 Fabrication Pipeline
To enable the rapid fabrication of V-Hook, we used a fused deposi-
tion modeling (FDM) 3D printer, the Prusa Mk3S+, equipped with a
standard 0.4 mm nozzle, ensuring the accessibility and reproducibil-
ity of our method. After preliminary tests, and considering the
balance between stiffness and elasticity, we selected PolyMax PLA
as the printing material.
By adjusting the extrusion amount and the printhead travel
speed, the extruded filament deforms under gravity during sus-
pended extrusion(fig. 2), forming a smooth and symmetricfull hook
Morphology. By further shifting the relative positions of the start
and end points, we can fabricatetilted hookstructures with direc-
tional angles. To create ahalf hook, we introduce a supporting wall
at the lowest point of deformation, allowing the extruded filament
to naturally sag and attach to the wall; the wall is then removed after
printing. Similarly, when three supporting walls of equal height are
used, a uniformdouble hookmorphology can be formed. Multiple
factors in this process can be parameterized, including the offset
angle, as well as the spacing and height of the supporting walls.
This substantially expands the diversity of hook morphologies, en-
abling systematic variation in parameters such as length, density,
angle, and proportion.
3.2 Motion Principle
Under vibration excitation, the hook structures interact with the
contact surface through asymmetric friction and stick–slip effects,
resulting in directional motion(fig. 2). Fortilted,full, anddouble
hooks, the motion direction generally aligns with the bending di-
rection of the hook. In contrast, thehalf hooktends to move in the
opposite direction. Meanwhile, because thedouble hookstructure
is mechanically similar to a spring, it exhibits greater elasticity dur-
ing motion and shows a stronger upward motion tendency under
vibration excitation.

with a footprint of 48 mm × 48 mm, a10 × 10density configuration,

4.2 Basic Testing
With the output voltage fixed at 90 V, we measured the motion
accuracy of the standard prototype (without additional load) under
different output frequencies. The results show that as frequency
increases, the motion accuracy first improves and then decreases.
In other words, there exists a frequency, or a range of frequencies,
at which the prototype can maintain motion accuracy above 90%,
and in some cases even approach 100%, corresponding to nearly
perfectly straight motion.
By progressively increasing the load, we found that motion ac-
curacy gradually decreased as the payload increased, and dropped
markedly beyond 270 g. Noticeable deformation of the hook mor-
phology was observed after 250 g, and beyond 270 g the hooks
could no longer maintain their intended shape. Although the pro-
totype was still capable of movement, its motion accuracy became
extremely low. The tested 15 mm module（Best load-bearing perfor-
mance） weighed only 3.8 g, yet was able to transport payloads of
up to 270 g under vibration, corresponding to a payload-to-weight
ratio of approximately71:1. In addition, compression tests showed
that the structure could withstand forces of up to 140 N before being
fully flattened, indicating both high dynamic payload capacity and
strong mechanical robustness.
4.3 Experiments, Findings and Evaluations
4.3.1 Different Hook Morphologies.Under the same hook length
and density, all four morphologies were able to maintain relatively
high motion accuracy. However, thehalf hookperformed slightly
worse than the other morphologies. We speculate that this is be-
cause its greater structural degree of freedom makes it more prone
to deformation in multiple directions under vibration, thereby intro-
ducing larger motion error. By contrast, thedouble hook, due to its
spring-like structural characteristics, exhibited stronger elasticity
and a more pronounced upward motion tendency, while thefull
hookshowed more prominent load-bearing performance.
4.3.2 Different Densities.The density experiments show that as
hook density increases, the load-bearing capacity of the structure
improves significantly, while the frequency range associated with
high motion accuracy gradually narrows, and the motion accuracy
at the same frequency decreases. In practice, at lower densities,
the local interaction between the hooks and the contact surface
is clearer and more stable, with less off-axis disturbance, making
directional motion easier to maintain. As density increases, struc-
tural interference among hooks becomes stronger. Although the
overall support capacity is improved, motion error is more likely
to be introduced.
4.3.3 Different Lengths.As hook length increases, motion accu-
racy gradually improves within a certain range, and the frequency
range over which high accuracy can be maintained shifts toward
higher frequencies, while the load-bearing capacity gradually de-
creases. However, this improvement does not continue indefinitely.
When the hook length reaches 40 mm, with the base size fixed, the
structural stiffness becomes insufficient, the hooks can no longer
remain upright, support fails, and motion accuracy drops sharply.
4.4 Summary
Taken together, the parametric studies in this chapter validate the
basic feasibility of V-Hook as a morphology-based locomotion prim-
itive. The results(fig. 4) show that fundamental morphologies deter-
mine motion modes, while parameter variations regulate motion
performance. At the same time, different performance metrics are
always linked by quantifiable trade-offs. V-Hook can achieve pre-
dictable and tunable motion behavior under simple vibration input,
and such tunability does not rely on complex electronic control,
but instead emerges from a parameter space structured by contact
Morphology and morphological design, in which motion accuracy,
load-bearing capacity, structural stability, and printability must be
balanced.
5 Design Space
5.1 Integrating Multiple Morphologies on a
Single Base
Our parametric studies revealed that different hook morphologies,
densities, and lengths exhibit different sensitivities to vibration
input. Building on this insight, we further explored the possibility
of integrating multiple hook morphologies on a single base. By ad-
justing the positions of supporting walls and the printing paths, we
successfully fabricated multiple hook types on the same substrate.
This all-in-one strategy allows a single V-Hook prototype to em-
bed multiple motion responses within one structure. Under different
vibration intensities, different hook morphologies can take on dom-
inant roles during motion, thereby producing different movement
behaviors without replacing the entire module. In this way, motion
variation is no longer achieved only by switching between sepa-
rate prototypes, but can also be encoded directly into a single base
through spatially distributed hook configurations. This expands
both the compactness and expressiveness of V-Hook, while sug-
gesting a new direction for designing multi-behavior locomotion
systems under shared vibration input.

5.2 Modifying the Base: From 2D Motion to 3D
Motion
Within a safe and comfortable vibration range, V-Hook prototypes
typically exhibit different forms of motion on a two-dimensional
plane. To further expand their motion capabilities, we modified the
base structure to shift the overall center of mass of the prototype.
Under inertial effects, even at relatively low vibration intensities,
V-Hook was able to extend its motion repertoire from planar 2D
motion to a range of 3D motion behaviors.
Rather than changing the hook structures themselves, this ap-
proach expands motion possibilities by redesigning the relation-
ship between the hook layer and the body above it. By introducing
three-dimensional base Morphologies, tilted mass distributions, or
elevated structures, the prototype can leverage the same vibration
input to generate rolling, rocking, and lifting tendencies in addition
to directional planar locomotion. This result suggests that V-Hook
can support not only motion design at the contact layer, but also mo-
tion transformation through body-level structural variation, further
broadening the design space from surface locomotion to volumetric
motion expression.
5.3 Connecting Modules for Motion
Composition
To further support user creativity and expand the design space, we
built on the tested single-unit morphologies and motion patterns to
enable modular combination through hinges, adhesives, and other
connection methods. Through such modular composition, users can
construct richer combinations of motion and interaction patterns
based on existing morphologies and behaviors.
This modular strategy enables motion to be designed not only
at the level of individual hook units, but also at the level of their
relationships and assemblies. For example, different modules can
be arranged to produce sequential, coupled, or competing motion
behaviors, allowing a system to translate, rotate, bend, or reorient
in more complex ways than a single unit alone. As a result, V-Hook
becomes more than an isolated locomotion primitive: it also serves
as a compositional building block for constructing richer motion
repertoires. Such modularity opens up opportunities for scalable
design exploration, where users can reuse, combine, and adapt
existing motion modules to create new behaviors with relatively
low fabrication overhead.
6 Design Tool
6.1 Software Tool
To help users better understand the fabrication process of V-Hook,
establish controllable and predictable mappings between morphol-
ogy and motion, and support customized design, we developed a
low-barrier software tool(fig. 6) based on Rhinoceros, Grasshopper,
and its interface plugin Human UI.
The tool packages complex fabrication and motion knowledge
into an operational design workflow. Users can first select a prede-
fined base or import a custom model and specify its bottom surface.
After defining the object weight and hook type, the system auto-
matically generates the corresponding parametric design interface.
For different morphologies, the tool provides reasonable param-
eter ranges related to vibration-driven motion, such as printable
density ranges, hook lengths that balance stiffness and elasticity,
and suitable angles and proportions for different motion patterns.
After completing the design of a single unit, users can further cre-
ate modular combinations within the software and preview the
expected motion through animation. Finally, the tool outputs the
final structural configuration and fabrication parameters to support
subsequent printing and assembly.

In addition to the local software tool, we further developed an

an encoding of vibration-driven motion behavior.

patterns and application examples, and further adapt existing de-

form aims to provide an accessible, comparable, and reusable online
resource for building a broader community around V-Hook.
7 Applications
7.1 Turning Everyday Vibration into Visible
Motion
In everyday life, vibration is commonly used as functional feedback,
while V-Hook transforms it into visible and understandable physical
motion. As shown in Fig. 8a, for makers and designers working
with 3D printing, V-Hook can be directly integrated into the bottom
surface of printed objects, allowing the vibration generated by
the printer itself to rapidly animate prototypes and thus connect
fabrication with motion generation in a single workflow. As shown
in Fig. 8b, when attached to a speaker, V-Hook can translate music
vibrations into rhythmic and expressive movement, making abstract
audio signals physically perceivable. As shown in Fig. 8c, when
embedded into a phone case, V-Hook can also extend smartphone
vibration into more spatially expressive behaviors, such as moving
the phone closer during an incoming call or rotating during an
alarm.

8 Discussion and Future Work
8.1 Morphology as a Design Variable
V-Hook proposes that motion outcomes are not determined pri-
marily by complex control, but are directly shaped by morpholog-
ical parameters. The parametric studies further validate that the
motion behavior of V-Hook can be systematically tuned, rather
than remaining at the level of empirical trial and error. However,
the current design space of V-Hook is still mainly built on macro-
level morphological parameters that can clearly distinguish motion
categories, while more fine-grained unit-level Morphologie s and
printing parameters are reflected more as continuous adjustments
to existing motion behaviors. Examples include the influence of
extrusion amount and printing speed on hook thickness and cur-
vature, the influence of different tilt angles (e.g., 30◦, 45◦, and 60◦)
on directional bias and structural support, and proportion-related
issues, such as where ahalf hookis truncated, the size ratio between
the two hooks in adouble hook, and whether the structure can be
extended into more continuous hooks.
However, under the current experimental conditions, the motion-
mode changes introduced by these parameters still show strong
convergence overall. They more often affect the intensity, stabil-
ity, and error distribution within the same class of motion behav-
ior, rather than introducing fundamentally different new motion
modes. Future work could further establish a higher-resolution
morphology–motion mapping around these parameters, analyze
how they affect dynamic metrics such as speed, trajectory stabil-
ity, jumping height, and contact recovery capability, and further
integrate this knowledge into the design tool.
8.2 Accuracy, Stability, and Load-Bearing
Capacity
The baseline experiments show that V-Hook can achieve relatively
high motion accuracy under simple vibration input and maintain
stable performance across a relatively wide frequency range. For
the standard prototype, when the output voltage was fixed at 90 V,
motion accuracy remained above 90% over a certain frequency
interval, and in some cases approached perfectly straight motion.
This suggests that contact Morphology design can compensate,
to some extent, for the inherent uncertainty of vibration-driven
motion, thereby improving motion stability and predictability.
In addition to motion accuracy, V-Hook also exhibits strong load-
bearing capacity, which is closely related to the printing material. In
the current work, we primarily focus on PLA. Its key advantage lies
in the fact that the stiffness and elasticity of the hooks can be dy-
namically shaped during fabrication through parameter adjustment.
Experimental results show that, compared with TPU, PLA achieves
higher motion accuracy and stronger load-bearing performance
under the current prototype scale and vibration conditions, suggest-
ing that higher stiffness helps maintain the stability of hook-based
contact Morphology. At the same time, PLA is also more prone to
brittle fracture or irreversible deformation during post-processing
and long-term use, and its durability and recoverability are weaker

8.3 Vibration Input and Motion Boundaries
The experiments in this work were primarily conducted on a vibra-
tion platform, while in the application section we further demon-
strated the possibility of other vibration sources such as speakers,
3D printers, and external tapping. These explorations suggest that
V-Hook has a certain degree of generality with respect to vibra-
tion input, but its motion performance still strongly depends on
the intensity, continuity, and coupling condition of the vibration
source. In practice, small vibration motors or lightweight actuators
often cannot provide sufficient driving force to fully activate the
motion potential of V-Hook. Conversely, achieving more dramatic
motion effects, such as obvious jumping, usually requires stronger
vibration intensity, which may also introduce noticeable discomfort.
Instantaneous excitation generated by forceful table tapping can
likewise induce momentary jumps due to the lightweight structure
of V-Hook. This suggests that its response is not limited to continu-
ous vibration, but may also extend to impulsive excitation, offering
a possible way to balance vibration input and practical application
needs.
Meanwhile, contact conditions constitute another important mo-
tion boundary for V-Hook. Our experiments show that as long as
stable surface contact can be established and effective asymmetric
friction can be generated, different hook morphologies can gener-
ally maintain relatively high motion accuracy. However, surface

roughness significantly affects motion speed: in general, smoother
surfaces tend to correspond to higher motion velocity. By contrast,
in outdoor environments such as sand and water, the standard
module (with a 20 mm hook length) performs substantially worse,
because the structure tends to sink into granular media or float on
the liquid surface, making it difficult to establish sufficient contact
and friction. However, when the hook length is increased, the struc-
ture can re-establish more effective contact and friction, and motion
accuracy correspondingly recovers. This suggests that limitations
introduced by the contact environment can, to some extent, still be
mitigated through adjustments to morphological parameters.
However, the present work still provides only limited investiga-
tion of vibration input itself. For example, the relationship between
controller output parameters and the actual mechanical amplitude
and acceleration of the platform has not yet been systematically
modeled. At the same time, our characterization of the morphology–
motion relationship is still mainly focused on motion accuracy and
motion mode, while more fine-grained dynamic metrics, such as ve-
locity, acceleration, trajectory stability, and jumping height, remain
underexplored. Future work could combine more refined experi-
mental design, motion capture, and modeling methods to establish a
more complete morphology–motion mapping and further improve
the ability of the design tool to support target motion behaviors.
9 Conclusion
We presented V-Hook as a morphology-driven approach to vibration-
driven locomotion design. Rather than relying on added control
complexity, V-Hook shows that motion can be systematically shaped
through hook morphology and parametric fabrication. Through
evaluation, design space exploration, and application prototypes,
V-Hook offers a new perspective for integrating motion capabilities
into digitally fabricated interactive systems.
